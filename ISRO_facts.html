Exploring ISRO: India's Space Achievements

History of ISRO

The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) was founded on August 15, 1969, by Dr. Vikram Sarabhai, the father of the Indian Space Programme. Before ISRO, the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) was established in 1962 under the leadership of Dr. Sarabhai and Dr. Homi Bhabha.

ISRO's main mission is to harness space technology for India's development and to conduct advanced space research.

ISRO's First Launch

ISRO launched its first satellite, Aryabhata, on April 19, 1975, with the help of the Soviet Union. Named after the legendary Indian astronomer, Aryabhata was an experimental satellite used for atmospheric and astronomical research.

Milestones Achieved by ISRO

  • Rohini Satellite: On July 18, 1980, India launched the Rohini satellite using its Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-3), marking a significant achievement.
  • Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV): Known for its reliability, PSLV successfully launched IRS-P2 into orbit on October 15, 1994.
  • Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV): The GSLV, first launched successfully on April 18, 2001, places satellites into geostationary orbits.
  • Chandrayaan-1: India's first Moon mission, launched on October 22, 2008, discovered water molecules on the lunar surface.
  • Mars Orbiter Mission (Mangalyaan): Launched on November 5, 2013, India became the first country to reach Mars orbit on its first attempt.
  • Navigation with Indian Constellation (NAVIC): NAVIC, with seven satellites, provides accurate real-time navigation services in India and nearby regions.
  • 104 Satellites in a Single Launch: ISRO set a world record on February 15, 2017, by launching 104 satellites in one mission.
  • Chandrayaan-3: India achieved a historic Moon landing on the south pole with the successful Chandrayaan-3 mission on August 23, 2023.
  • Aditya-L1: Launched on September 2, 2023, this mission studies the Sun using advanced payloads.

Interesting Facts about ISRO

Cost-Effective Missions: The Mars Orbiter Mission cost only $74 million, showcasing ISRO's expertise in affordable space exploration.
Women Power: Women scientists have played critical roles in missions like the Mars Orbiter Mission and Chandrayaan-2.
International Collaborations: ISRO has partnered with NASA, CNES, and other space agencies for joint missions and projects.
Reusable Launch Vehicle (RLV): ISRO is developing a reusable launch vehicle to reduce satellite launch costs significantly.
Space Education: Initiatives like the Young Scientist Programme (Yuvika) aim to inspire the next generation of space enthusiasts.
